// artist
Born in the vibrant yet excruciating chaos of Dhaka, Nuzhat Minhaz is a Bangladeshi artist whose work explores the shapeshifting and conflicting nature of identity through charcoal, graphite, and the occasional defiant splash of red. She draws figures who are neither fully here nor fully there — women caught between belonging and longing, strength and grief, visibility and erasure.
Art teacher at 17. National athlete at 16. Cybersecurity startup founder at 19. Tech PM at 22. Solo exhibit by the Seattle waterfront at 26. She is not one thing. That has always been the point.
"I have always drawn what I couldn't say. The figures in my work are not portraits of absence — they are portraits of the weight of being present in a world that keeps demanding you translate yourself."
Her mediums — charcoal, graphite 6B and 8B, ink and brush, acrylic — are chosen for what they resist. Charcoal smears. Graphite ghosts. Ink doesn't forgive. Acrylic covers what came before, eventually. The body in her work is always doing something with this resistance: reaching, concealing, standing with its back turned, dissolving.

I have been called Bideshini — a woman from a foreign land. Unreachable because my thoughts can never fully translate, and even when they do, the translation carries something that isn't quite the feeling anymore. I have been called this with love, with frustration, with a kind of wistful resignation that says: you are too far away now. You went too far.
"This show is my passport of contradictions. It is the archive of what I carried, what I couldn't leave behind, and what I became in the distance between."
These 16 works were made across three countries and a decade of displacement. Not all displacement is painful. Some of it is chosen. Some of it is the price you pay for becoming who you needed to be. But all of it leaves a mark — in the way you hold your body in a room, in what you reach for and what you conceal, in what you know in your bones that you cannot explain in the language of wherever you currently are standing.
The figures in these works are not me. They are all of me. The girl who got on a plane alone. The woman who won who knew something was being lost in the winning. The body that learned to be beautiful in grief. I don't know how to resolve these contradictions. I only know how to draw them.

Softness as a form of danger. Grace that has learned something the world didn't intend to teach it.
Where It Hurts She Heals
2025  ·  Charcoal
Not recovery. Not resolution. The specific ache of healing in the exact place the wound was. Same location, different quality of pain.
Her Back to the World
2023  ·  Ink
Not defeat. A choice. The figure faces away because she has earned the right to stop performing her own presence.
Untethered

What freedom looks like when it wasn't chosen. The body in open space, and whether it reads as liberation or abandon.
Displacement
2025  ·  Charcoal

The Weight of Skin
2025  ·  Graphite 6B 8B
6B for shadow. 8B for the places where shadow becomes something closer to erasure. The heaviness isn't metaphor.
Rehearsal for Presence
2025  ·  Charcoal
Practice. The figure is practicing being here. Being visible. Being enough in a room that didn't build itself around her.

Rupture
2025  ·  Acrylic
The first acrylic in the series. Color because some breaks aren't grey. Some are loud and warm and cannot be contained in monochrome.

2025  ·  Acrylic

2022  ·  Acrylic on canvas  ·  after Elly Smallwood
Made in conversation with Elly Smallwood's submerged figures. Submersion without surrender. The slow kind — where you can still see the surface light.
